No Gordian Knot is untangleable, just ask Alexander the Great!

The aspects of the solution are simple: there is a requirement for a diagnostic test that needs to be performed only once followed by an accurate estimate of whether the cancer cell is benign or malignant.

Fortunately, a more accurate diagnostic test does exist. Namely, the FNAC (Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology) test. The procedure involves removal of cells from the suspicious region of a breast with the help of a syringe.

Despite what it seems like this isn’t a painful process with interviews suggestive of an experience akin to that of a regular vaccination. The FNAC test is also said to be statistically and clinically more efficacious.

Let’s compare the two tests: technically speaking diagnostic tests are graded based on their performance in four categories, namely Sensitivity, Specificity, Positive Predictive Value (PPV) and Negative Predictive Value (NPV). These terms have been explained in easy, informal terms here.

Note that the percentage value is directly proportional to the efficiency/performance of the test itself i.e. the higher the better.

Sensitivity for the Mammogram test stands at 77.77% while it is 97.22% for the FNAC test. Mammograms have a specificity of about 97.22% and FNAC has a Specificity of about 99.46%. The PPV for Mammograms is 87.5% and stands at about 97.22% for the FNAC test. The NPV on the other hand is 95.55% and 99.46% for Mammograms and FNAC tests respectively.

Characteristic Mammogram FNAC
Sensitivity 77.77% 97.22%

PPV 87.5% 97.22%

The numbers don’t lie! The FNAC is indeed superior to the Mammogram when it comes to diagnostic prowess. Then, why isn’t FNAC used more widely considering the fact that in terms of accuracy it seems to trump mammography in every regard. The answer leads us back to our second problem: the lack of skilled and experienced doctors. Although a test may perform with high accuracy the evaluation of the result is highly dependent on the acumen of the cytologist. Keeping in mind the shortage of doctors in this field, the diagnostic accuracy of doctors range between 77-99%, in other words it is impractical to expect a consistently accurate diagnosis (again, not every hospital/Breast Cancer Treatment centre can be expected to have a skilled doctor on duty).

A combination of a FNAC/Mammogram test is often asked from the patient. But there are also follow up tests, requests for more scans etc which once again falls heavy on your wallet. Sometimes doctors take too long (upto 2 weeks or more) to arrive at a conclusion. The problem here is that humans are confined by constraints and can’t think in multiple dimensions. While skilled doctors may find it easy to cure cancer, detection is always the problem.

This is where Patalah steps in. Based on the values obtained from the FNAC test of a breast mass our AI model will predict whether your cancer is benign or malignant. Keeping the Standard Deviation in mind our algorithm has an accuracy of 95-99% while as mentioned above doctors have a diagnostic accuracy of 77-99%.

An AI is not liable to any constraints and can think in multiple dimensions thus overcoming the problem that prevents accurate detection.
